French Bulldog Adoption Guide

AKA: Frenchies, make for great companions. This adoption guide will help you make the best choice for your family when adopting a Frenchie.

In this sample webpage, I plan to showcase some of my progress using the information I learned while a French bulldog parent. This information is strictly for portfolio purposes and space-filling. While it may have truth to it, it is not to be the focus of my website.

We've come to love Frenchies because they made great companies have cute bat ears, and their personalities are out of this world- but what does it mean to love a Frenchie? What kind of care will they need, and how do you choose the right Frenchie for your family and budget? After all, this breed is truly an investment, and it's important to support healthy breeding and not puppy mills.


AKC vs. Not AKC:

//insert photo of AKC Certificate

Does AKC (American Kennel Association) certification matter? The answer is no. The regulations of the AKC are strict and mostly there for competition purposes. A blue Frenchie, for example, would not qualify to be an AKC registered dog but would still make a great addition to your home.

Color Choices:

  • Cream


    This coloring is supposedly more sensitive to the elements, and I have found this true with my cream-colored Frenchie. He is more allergy prone and gets cold a lot easier than my brindle Frenchie. He also breaks out in boils if exposed to his allergies and doesn't heal as quickly. While this was my initial desired Frenchie coat color, I would have selected another color if I had a time machine because this poor little dude suffers a lot. We love him; he was born regardless, so we will always have a good solid home.

  • Brindle


    I swear this one is my larger Frenchie; he always looks smaller. The coat coloring is very slimming.

  • Brown


    This one, I would assume, is the healthiest coat and least prone to health problems.

Posture



I'm mostly going to touch on the nose here. Having as long of a snout as possible is essential to help with breathing problems later. If you don't mind shelling out nearly (to sometimes over) three thousand dollars for a corrective surgery later down the line, this may not be something to concern yourself with. Even with pet health insurance, which many will have a clause not to cover this surgery because of the specific breed, you'll still be spending a pretty penny or watching your pup suffer.

Supplies

  1. Crate and crate mat (they have small bladders, so crating at night helps accidents and tends to teach them to hold it longer)
  2. Proper healthy dog food (this breed is allergy-prone, and good healthy vet-recommended dog food is the way to go with keeping a nice coat and a happy, healthy dog).
  3. Water and food bowls (I recommend a water bowl that refills itself- we have a 2-gallon bowl that needs washing and filling once a week. It helps keep things simple.)
  4. Harnesses and a leash. There are so many cute options that we own several like someone else might own several outfits. Some people like to dress their Frenchies up, so that you might include clothing here, but we don’t find the need to do that.
  5. Shampoo and Conditioner for their coats
  6. Toys (we like soft toys but be careful because they can be ripped apart and pose a choking hazard. We watch them closely and put the toys away after playtime).

Common Problems:

  • IVDD:

    IVDD stands for ###. This can be tested genetically and has a possibility of correction via expensive surgeries. Currently, one of our Frenchies has it, and we are managing it by keeping his weight down via dieting and daily walks. We are also limiting his jumping on the couch. We have found that he moves around like an old man, even though he’s not very old.

  • Breathing:

    Our pup with IVDD also has breathing issues and could use corrective surgery. His nose is just a tiny bit shorter than our cream Frenchie's nose, which makes a huge difference. We help him by taking breaks on our walks, a stroller for a more extended vacation, and a cooling harness.

  • Allergies:

    Frenchies are prone to allergies. I don't think ours were ethically bred, so they are actively struggling with health issues. The allergies are remedied with monthly allergy shots, daily allergy pills, and steroids. They are constantly getting hot spots and are itchy. It has proven to be a costly health issue. Our pups didn't experience allergies until we moved to southeast Texas. It was dry enough in the Colorado mountains, and they weren't allergic to anything. We could feed them just about anything we wanted without itchy coats. The environmental allergies, humid temperatures, and paired diet have just been too much for them here in Texas. We have had to change their diet completely, start long-term, expensive medical treatment, and wash them every four days to help keep their allergies at bay. We also have to treat our yard to help keep the fungus and allergens at bay. It's essential to remember this when choosing to adopt a Frenchie, as the costs increase. If you cannot afford to maintain your Frenchie (and we have two), then they will suffer and could potentially die.

    For example, our cream Frenchie had developed boils (two of them) from outdoor allergens. The spots then became infected almost overnight. We weren't sure what was going on. Had we not sought after medical treatment, he would have become septic and died from the infection. Thankfully, we met with our vet and had a careful treatment plan which included many shots of antibiotics and steroids paired with more antibiotic and allergy pills. Then we started washing with special prescription shampoos and conditioners every couple of days. The whole thing was such a nightmare, but we are so happy that he survived it and is healing. His coat will probably never look the same because of all the boils and skin infection scaring. We also used a special ointment and have been treating him for months. He is happy and healthy, but it has been a journey.

Overall

I will continue to adopt and raise Frenchies as they are my favorite breed, but I have learned a lot after adopting my current set of Frenchies. I hope this information helps you decide which Frenchie you'd like to add to your family.
